AN INTRODUCTION TO
WEBSITE ACCESSIBILITY

Website accessibility is the practice of creating websites that are usable for all visitors regardless of disability or impairment. This means ensuring that websites are designed and coded in a way that people with disabilities can use them by adjusting format, structure, navigation, visuals, and written content. Web accessibility is monitored and enforced by members of the Web Accessibility Initiative (WIP) of The World Wide Web Constortium (W3C). These organizations publish the Web Content Accessibility guidlines (WCAG) that we'll be discussing today. So, why is this so important? The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that 16% of the global population has a disability. By following the design and development guidelines provided on this website, you can ensure that your website is built to accomodate anyone.

Who does web accessibility affect?

According to the WHO, one in six people have a disability. Choosing to ignore website accessibility guidelines means choosing to ignore an incredibly large chunk of individuals from engaging with your site. Inaccessible websites can create barriers that prevent people with disabilities from accessing information and services online. This exclusion violates the rights of a large population and limits their ability to participate in society. Web accessibility supports all disabilities that affect access to the web including:

  • Auditory: deafness and hearing loss
  • Congnitive: learning disabilities
  • Physical: limited mobility or dexterity
  • Speech: difficulty with speech recognition or production
  • Visual: blindness, low vision, and color blindness

Why should I care about website accessibility?

Creating a website that is accessible not only supports those with disabilities, but also allows more users overall to access your site. Adhering to accessibility guidlines demonstrates a company's commitment to inclusivity and shows that you care about the user experiences. It ensures equitable access to the web for all people, removing barriers that restrict certain groups from using the web and promoting diversity. Web accessibility is beneficial to all users, not just those with disabilities. Following accessibility guarantees clear navigation; well-organized content; text altertives for images for users who have slow internet connection; and captions in videos for user with noisy environments.

Who enforces website accessibility?

Government sites must abide by Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act, requiring federal agencies to make their electronic and information technology accessible to people with disabilities. This includes websites, software, and other digital content. Failure to comply wih Section 508 requirements can result in legal consequences for government agencies, including lawsuits and penalties. According to the Americans with disabilities Act (ADA) Standards for Accessibility design, all electronic and information technology must be accessible to people with disabilities. Private businesses face the risk of encountering legal and financial reprocussions for being inaccessible.
