WEB ACCESSIBILITY STANDARDS

According to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) published by the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), there are four main principles that constitute an accessible website: perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust.

1. Perceivable

Visitors of your website must be able to be aware and understand the content prestsented on your site. Keep in mind that those with blindness or low vision may use a screen reader software to convert text into sythesized speech. Consider the following ways that you can adjust your site to make it available to as many audiences as possible.

  1. All images, video, and audio elements must have a text alternative
  2. Include image alt text to all images
  3. Offer transcripts for audio and video recordings
  4. Content should be structured in a way that is easily adaptable
  5. Utilize color contrast and personalized audio settings

2. Operable

Users should be able to utilize all of functions of an operable website without disruptions. In addition, all visitors should be able to use every part of the site. It's important to keep your website simple and ditch excess functionality to maximize operability.

  1. Users should be able to navigate the entire site with the keyboard
  2. Allow users to engage with content within a reasonable time contraint
  3. Avoid blinking or flashing content
  4. Provide clear navigation

3. Understandable

It is crucial that your website can be understood by a wide range of users. Avoid making your site too verbose or jumbled and keep in mind those who don't speak your site's predominant language. Your site should be intuitively organized and navigation should be easily accessible. Providing a clear layout and design is necessary to accomodate all users.

  1. Writing should be clear and concise
  2. Pages should be structured logically
  3. Navigational links should be placed intuitively
  4. Include useful error messages

4. Robust

Websites must be written in HTML that can be parsed, meaning that elements have correct start and end tags, do not contain duplicate attributes, have unique ID's, and are organized according to their specifications. Improper markup can lead to content displaying differently on different browsers.

  1. Displayer content as the creator intends
  2. Functions as the creator intends
  3. Is compatible with current and future browsers
